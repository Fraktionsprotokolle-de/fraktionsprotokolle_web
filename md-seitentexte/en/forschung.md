# <p style="text-align: center;">Research Relevance</p>

---

## Source Value and Chronological Scope

The **records of the parliamentary party groups in the German Bundestag and of the CSU-Landesgruppe (regional group) from 1949 to 2005** constitute, taken together, a key source for the history of parliamentarism and political parties in Germany. They span the period from the early Federal Republic through the end of the “old” Federal Republic in 1990 to the “Berlin Republic,” thereby opening up a long phase of parliamentary development in Germany. From an internally differentiated party-political perspective, they document the internal processes of negotiation and decision-making within the parliamentary party groups and thus provide particularly dense insight into the political practice of the Bundestag. Through its **digital publication**, the edition offers historians as well as political and social scientists a broad empirical basis for a wide range of research questions.

## Parliamentary Party Groups as Key Institutions of Parliamentary Decision-Making

On the basis of this extensive body of material, political developments in the Federal Republic can be re-examined from both diachronic and comparative perspectives. The parliamentary party groups emerge here as central arenas in which social interests are taken up, politically processed, and translated into processes of consensus-building or conflict management. The records are therefore of particular importance not only for party history, but also for the **study of the German Bundestag as an institution**. Within the functionally differentiated parliamentary system, the parliamentary party groups bundle key processes of communication, coordination, and decision-making; at the same time, they maintain diverse relationships with political actors and institutions at the federal and state levels. The sources document both the everyday practice of parliamentary work and exceptional political constellations, thereby opening up new possibilities for assessing more precisely the influence, autonomy, and room for manoeuvre of governing and opposition parliamentary party groups.

## Parliamentary Culture, Political Language, and Digital Analysis

For the **study of parliamentary culture**, the verbatim minutes and tape transcripts preserved for the CDU/CSU and SPD parliamentary party groups are of particular value. They provide dense insight into the self-conception, performance, and communicative practices of political actors, while also making visible role expectations, forms of political staging, and the relationship between internal deliberation, media orientation, and the public sphere. In addition, changes in political language can be traced more precisely in these records than, for example, in the editorially revised plenary proceedings of the Bundestag. The edition is therefore relevant not only to contemporary history and political science, but also to research in linguistics and communication history. By encoding all documents in the **human- and machine-readable TEI-XML format** and by making the research data openly available, the sources become systematically searchable, comparable, and reusable for digital methods of analysis.

Their particular value for **biographical and prosopographical research** should also be emphasized. The unambiguous identification of the persons mentioned in the records and their linkage to authority data make it possible to examine not only the leadership elites of the parliamentary party groups, but also “ordinary” members of parliament, experts, interest representatives, and political dissenters in greater detail. In this way, the edition provides a solid basis for group-specific portraits, generational and gender profiles, and for further **research questions in Digital History and historically oriented Computational Humanities**. At the same time, its scholarly value does not lie solely in the provision of sources and data.

The edition also generates research in its own right: in the scholarly introductions to the printed volumes, which often go well beyond mere editorial contextualization, as well as in articles, workshops, and conferences in which questions relating to contemporary history, methodology, and scholarly editing are examined further.
