# <p style="text-align: center;">Research Relevance</p>

------



The minutes of the parliamentary groups of the German Bundestag from 1949, through the end of the »old« Federal Republic in 1990, and into the »Berlin Republic« up to 2005 constitute, as a corpus, an outstanding source for the history of parliamentarism and political parties. In their chronological scope, they uniquely document –through an internally differentiated, party-specific perspective – the functioning of a modern parliament, including its thematic negotiation and decision-making processes. With their online publication, an indispensable empirical foundation is made available to historians, political scientists, and social scientists for a wide range of scholarly inquiries. At the same time, the complete edition also serves pedagogical purposes by contributing to a fundamental understanding of democracy in Germany up to the present day and by enhancing the transparency of political decision-making processes.

On the basis of this material, it is particularly promising to illuminate fields of research in the history of the Federal Republic – and, by extension, European history – through both cross-sectional and longitudinal analyses. The parliamentary groups, especially with regard to their reflection of societal interests and the mechanisms of consensus-building and conflict management, function as indicators and resonance spaces of social change and transformations in the political culture of the Federal Republic of Germany.

The minutes of the parliamentary group meetings are of central importance for research on the German Bundestag as an institution within the multi-level political system of the Federal Republic. In the division-of-labor structure of the Bundestag, many threads converge within the parliamentary groups as decisive arenas of action. In turn, the parliamentary groups maintain manifold relationships with political actors and institutions at both federal and state levels. The sources illustrate everyday parliamentary practice as well as exceptional political events. Any assessment of the Bundestag as a factor of power necessarily raises the question of the influence and autonomy of both governing and opposition parliamentary groups.

For the study of parliamentary culture, the detailed verbatim minutes and tape transcripts available for the CDU/CSU and SPD parliamentary groups are particularly revealing. In their largely unfiltered immediacy, they provide access to the performance and self-perception of political actors, as well as to political staging, role expectations, and forms of communication in interaction with the media and the public. Moreover, these minutes – more clearly than the edited plenary proceedings of the Bundestag – make it possible to trace changes in political language, thereby opening a fruitful field of research for linguistics as well. Through the encoding of all documents in the human- and machine-readable TEI-XML format and the provision of all research data on [GitHub](https://github.com/Fraktionsprotokolle-de) and [Zenodo](https://zenodo.org/doi/10.5281/zenodo.6552508), long-term and broad interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary reusability is ensured.

Not least, the documents offer a wealth of starting points for biographical and prosopographical studies. Through the unambiguous identification of all persons mentioned in the minutes and their linkage to authority data, attention is directed not only to the »ordinary« members of parliament but also to factional leadership elites, experts, interest representatives, and political dissenters. The edition provides a solid basis for group-specific portraits, generational analyses, and gender profiles.
